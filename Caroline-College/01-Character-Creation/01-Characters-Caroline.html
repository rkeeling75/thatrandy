    01-Setting Rules - Caroline College

    House Rules for Caroline College - An Early 1980's Call of Cthulhu Campaign Setting

    These house rules are designed to enhance your gaming experience by:

    • Increasing character survivability regarding sanity loss.
    • Providing more development options for characters.
    • Keeping starting character abilities aligned with their status as beginning college students.
    • Adding more depth to characters through enriched backstories and mechanics.

    Disclaimer

    This is a game of horror. Some themes may be disturbing to some players. Players are required to engage with the Session Zero to ensure that the game is appropriate for themselves, all players, and the keeper at the table.

    Character Creation

    Determining Character Stats

    Cap on Starting Skill Values

    • No starting skill can be above 60% after modifications.

    Rolling and Distributing Characteristics

    For Caroline College, we use a variant of Option 3: Choosing Where to Place Rolled Characteristics.

    1. Roll 6 Ã 4d4 and distribute the results across:
      • Strength (STR)
      • Constitution (CON)
      • Dexterity (DEX)
      • Appearance (APP)
      • Power (POW)
      • Luck (LUCK)
    2. Roll 3 Ã (3d4+4) and distribute the results across:
      • Size (SIZ)
      • Intelligence (INT)
      • Education (EDU)

    Note on Skill Caps: To reflect the inexperience of college freshmen, the cap on starting skills is set at 60%. This ensures characters have room for noticeable growth throughout the campaign.

    Recommendation: Playing a character â 39 years old is thematically appropriate. But nothing is stopping you from making a feisty middle-ager or spunky retiree. Just work with the party to explain why these generationally disparate people are spending so much time together.

    Occupation: College Student

    All characters at Caroline College use the occupation College Student.

    • Occupation Skill Points: EDU Ã 3 (Reduced from the standard to reflect starting students but with more opportunities for advancement during the campaign).
    • Credit Rating: 5â10
    • Suggested Contacts: Other students.
    • Skills:
      • Language (Own or Other)
      • Library Use
      • Listen
      • Three Fields of Study (choose three academic skills relevant to your character's studies)
      • Any Two Other Skills as personal or era specialties

    Backstory Elements

    Developing a rich backstory adds depth to your character and enhances the gaming experience. Each character should create entries for the following areas, aiming for meaningful and engaging content. This is a horror game, so dark elements within a backstory is acceptable, thematically appropriate, and strongly encouraged (within the limits defined by Session Zero). Try to remember that your characters don't know what's happening in the setting yet; hints and suggestions go a long way!

    Ideology/Beliefs

    • Bad Example: "I believe in justice."
      • Why it's bad: Too vague and generic; lacks depth.
    • Acceptable Example: "I believe that the legal system is flawed, and true justice can only be achieved outside of it."
      • Why it's acceptable: Adds complexity and hints at potential conflicts.
    • Good Example: "Ethan prowls the city's backstreets at night, where justice rarely reaches. When the courts failed him and the guilty walked free, he wasn't above bending the rules to set things right. To him, the law is just a guideline, and true justice sometimes requires a darker path that others might deem questionable."
      • Why it's good: Provides depth, aligns with game themes, and introduces moral ambiguity.

    Significant People

    • Bad Example: "My mother."
      • Why it's bad: Too general; doesn't explain significance.
    • Acceptable Example: "My mother, who supported my academic pursuits."
      • Why it's acceptable: Offers context but lacks depth.
    • Good Example: "Amelia carries a tattered letter from her estranged mother, filled with unsent words of apology. She reads it under dim lamplight, wondering about the life her mother chose over her family. Each word fuels Amelia's resolve to find her, not just to reconnect, but to understand the choices that led them apart."
      • Why it's good: Adds personal stakes, urgency, and ties to potential plot lines.

    Meaningful Locations

    • Bad Example: "The park."
      • Why it's bad: Too vague; lacks meaningful context.
    • Acceptable Example: "The old park near my childhood home where I used to play."
      • Why it's acceptable: Provides some context but lacks depth.
    • Good Example: "The rusted remains of the old Ferris wheel loom over the deserted fairground, a place where Lucas once shared a fleeting moment of joy with his first love before she moved away without a word. He returns there, the silence heavy with memories and unanswered questions, hoping one day to recapture that lost connection or at least find closure."
      • Why it's good: Rich atmosphere, ties into supernatural elements, and profoundly impacts the character.

    Treasured Possessions

    • Bad Example: "A book."
      • Why it's bad: Generic; doesn't reveal anything about the character.
    • Acceptable Example: "An old diary from my grandmother."
      • Why it's acceptable: Provides a familial connection but could be more detailed.
    • Good Example: "Lily discovered an old, leather-bound book hidden behind a loose brick in her grandmother's fireplace. The pages are filled with faded handwriting and strange symbols she doesn't understand. As she reads, unsettling family secrets begin to surfaceâstories of forgotten ancestors and choices that cast long shadows over her family's history, secrets they had tried desperately to keep buried. At least she knows she isn't alone in her bad choices."
      • Why it's good: Deeply personal, tied to game lore, and serves as a catalyst for adventures.

    Balancing Classes and Investigations

    Class Selection

    Players must balance academic responsibilities with their mystery-solving adventures.

    • Each Semester, players must choose 5 classes.

    Freshman Fall Semester:

    • 3 Required Classes:
      • One Science
      • One History
      • One Language
    • 2 Optional Classes:
      • Chosen from the list of electives.

    Passing a Class

    To pass a class, players must:

    1. Pass the Final Exam: - Involves making an advancement roll in the relevant topic.
      • Luck may be used on this roll.
    2. Optionally, Meet the Attendance Requirement: - Attend at least 70% of the class sessions (determined narratively).
      • Meeting attendance grants a +5% bonus to the relevant skill (in addition to advancements from exams).

    Exams

    • Mid-Term and Final Exams will be held each semester.
    • Passing the Mid-Term:
      • Grants a Penalty Die on the final exam advancement roll.
      • Note: In advancement rolls, a failed roll results in skill improvement. Therefore, the Penalty Die increases your chance to fail the roll, which benefits your skill advancement.
    • Failing a Class:
      • If you fail the final exam but met the attendance requirement, you still gain the +5% bonus.

    Tension Mechanic

    Players must choose between attending classes or pursuing adventures, balancing academic success with their investigations.

    • Skipping Classes may lead to:
      • Falling grades.
      • Loss of skill advancement opportunities.
      • Potential narrative consequences.
    • Benefits of Academic Success:
      • At the keeper's discretion, and at thematically appropriate times, academic achievements may grant benefits.
      • Example: Passing all exams in a semester might unlock access to exclusive resources, faculty support, or special permissions.

    Mechanics Adjustments

    Sanity Loss Adjustments

    • Reading Mythos Tomes:
      • Costs sanity but will not trigger bouts of madness or insanity if done in a safe environment.

    Recovery from Indefinite Insanity

    Students at Caroline College have access to counseling sessions provided by the local Lutheran church. These sessions are non-religious and aim to aid students in recovering from indefinite insanity and trauma.

    • Weekly Counseling Sessions:
      • Availability: Once per week when not participating in a game session.
      • Sanity Recovery Mechanics:
        • Recovery Attempt:
          • Roll percentile dice (1d100).
          • On a result of 01â95:
            • Success: Recover 1d10 Sanity Points.
          • On a result of 96â100:
            • Setback:
              • Lose 1d6 Sanity Points.
              • Gain a new phobia related to the stress or topics discussed during counseling.
        • Resolving Indefinite Insanity:
          • If Sanity Points are gained during a counseling session, you may immediately make a Sanity check (using your updated Sanity score).
          • On a Hard Success, your indefinite insanity is resolved.
      • Time Commitment:
        • Attending a counseling session requires an in-game time slot, potentially conflicting with classes or investigations.
      • Keeper's Note:
        • Frequent therapy sessions may draw attention or have narrative implications, adding to the tension of balancing time.
        • Failed Therapy Sessions:
          • May induce phobias related to the therapy content, providing additional story hooks.

    Luck Recovery and Advancement

    Luck advancements are made at the start of every game session.

    • Luck Recovery Mechanics:
      • Improvement Check:
        • Roll 1d100.
        • If the roll is above your current Luck, add 1d10 points.
        • If the roll is equal to or less than your current Luck, no points are recovered.
    • Note:
      • Luck may not exceed 99.
      • The starting Luck value is not used again in the game.

    Skill Advancements

    Skill advancements are made after thematically appropriate breaks, as determined by the keeper (expect 3â5 per semester, including exams).

    PvP Interactions and Loss of Control

    • Player vs. Player (PvP) 'Attacks':
      • Must be made with consent of all involved players.
    • Loss of Control Situations:
      • Occur when a character is under the effects of insanity, a Breakdown due to Distress, or certain supernatural influences.
      • Actions are at the keeper's discretion.
      • The keeper should consider player enjoyment and strive for consensual storytelling.

    Backstory Connections

    • Robust and Thematically Appropriate Backstories:
      • Include one or more entries with a dark element.
      • Develop meaningful locations, significant people, and pivotal events that add depth.
    • Character Connections:
      • Every character must have a connection to at least two other characters in the party in their backstory.
      • No character can have more than two connections to them.

    Group Skill Checks

    • Assisting with Skill Rolls:
      • If more than one character attempts a skill roll for the same task, under pressure:
        • If at least two characters have skill above baseline, one person can make the roll with a Bonus Die.

    Spending Luck

    • Luck Points can be spent to alter the result of a skill or characteristic roll on a 1-for-1 basis.
    • Restrictions:
      • Cannot spend Luck on:
        • Luck rolls
        • Damage rolls
        • Sanity rolls
        • Rolls to determine the amount of Sanity points lost
      • Cannot spend Luck on Pushed Rolls.
      • Criticals, Fumbles, and Firearm Malfunctions cannot be altered with Luck.
      • No skill improvement check is earned if Luck was used to alter the roll.

    Expanded Sanity Rules

    These OPTIONAL rules add psychological realism to the Sanity mechanics, fitting for modern or historical settings where characters may be hardy and accustomed to man-made horrors. Their inclusion will be discussed during Session Zero.

    Session Zero Guidelines

    Starting Campaigns

    We begin new campaigns with a Session Zero to:

    • Align expectations.
    • Discuss boundaries.
    • Establish the tone.
    • Integrate characters into the world.

    Backstories

    • Characters Should Not Start as Heroes:
      • Focus on backstories that plant seeds for future adventures.
      • Avoid having characters who have already achieved greatness.

    Fitting the Tone

    • Ensure your character fits the theme of the campaign (early 1980's horror sit in small town America).

    Party Dynamics

    • Create Characters That Fit the Party:
      • Develop strong connections with at least two other characters.
      • Collaborate during character creation to ensure cohesion.
      • Come to session zero with ideas, not characters.

    Respecting the Mood

    • Maintain Immersion:
      • Avoid breaking tension with out-of-place jokes, side talk, etc..
      • Engage with the core themes agreed upon.

    Rule of Cool

    • Story Over Rules: We'll follow the Rule of Cool to prioritize narrative over strict rule adherence.

    Improvisation and Player Agency

    • Your Actions Shape the Story:
      • Major events occur based on your decisions.
      • Creativity is rewarded, even if payoffs aren't immediate.
      • Not every encounter is balanced; sometimes avoiding conflict is wise.

    Player Preparation

    • Stay Engaged:
      • Be attentive during sessions.
      • Review notes between sessions.
      • Keep your character curious and involved.
    • Session Recaps:
      • Before each session, a player provides a recap of the previous session (group's choosing).
      • Incentive:
        • The player who provides the recap gains a one-time Bonus Die for the session (unused bonus dice do not roll over).

    Safety Tools

    • Player Comfort Is a Priority:
      • Session Zero: Establish comfort levels and boundaries.
      • Lines & Veils:
        • Lines: Hard limits not to be crossed.
        • Veils: Topics that can occur but not be spotlighted.
      • X Card:
        • Tap the X card to immediately stop and change uncomfortable content.
      • Consent Flowers:
        • Green: Keep going.
        • Yellow: Proceed with caution.
        • Red: Change the subject.
    • Examples:
      • A player indicating a yellow consent flower might prompt the keeper to adjust the scene's intensity without disrupting gameplay.

    Themes and Boundaries

    • Discuss Sensitive Topics:
      • Be proactive in expressing preferences.
      • Common topics include violence, discrimination, sexuality, and personal phobias.

    Conflict Resolution

    • In-Character Conflict:
      • Should serve the narrative.
      • Avoid actions that disrupt group dynamics.
    • Out-of-Character Conflict:
      • Address issues respectfully among yourselves.
      • The keeper is a storyteller, not a mediator.

    Role-Playing

    • Immerse Yourself:
      • Act and speak as your character.
      • Avoid phrases like "My character does X."

    Player Respect

    • Treat Each Other with Respect:
      • Respect beliefs and views without imposing them.
      • Create a welcoming environment.
      • Address discomfort promptly and respectfully.

    Common Topics to Discuss

    During Session Zero, we'll address the following possible story elements to ensure everyone's comfort:

    • Frequently Lined Topics:
      • Sexualized violence
      • Child abuse
      • Animal cruelty
    • Frequently Veiled Topics:
      • Racism
      • Discrimination against minorities
      • Sexuality
      • Torture
      • Phobias (spiders, snakes, clowns, dentists)
      • Serious illnesses
      • Sexism
      • Suicide

    By adhering to these house rules and guidelines, we aim to create an engaging, immersive, and enjoyable gaming experience for everyone involved. Let's work together to weave a memorable story set in the mysterious world of Caroline College.

    Clubs and Societies

    Below are 20 clubs and societies with you might engage with in and around the college. Each includes associated Call of Cthulhu skills.

    1. Great Lakes Archaeological Society
      • Skills: Archaeology, History
      • Story Hooks: Discovering Native American artifacts linked to ancient rituals; uncovering local historical sites.
    2. Caroline College Debate Club
      • Skills: Persuade, Fast Talk
      • Story Hooks: Debating controversial topics that stir up hidden tensions; uncovering faculty secrets.
    3. Peach Street Library Occult Studies Group
      • Skills: Occult, Library Use
      • Story Hooks: Access to obscure local folklore; accidental involvement with real supernatural forces.
    4. Folklore and Mythology Circle
      • Skills: Anthropology, History
      • Story Hooks: Exploring regional legends; finding connections between myths and current strange occurrences.
    5. Caroline Science Association
      • Skills: Science (choose one), Mechanical Repair
      • Story Hooks: Experiments affected by mysterious phenomena; encountering anomalies in nature.
    6. Literary Appreciation Society
      • Skills: Language (Own or Other), Art/Craft (Literature)
      • Story Hooks: Uncovering hidden messages in classic literature; meeting reclusive authors with dark secrets.
    7. Psychology and Wellness Club
      • Skills: Psychology, Psychoanalysis
      • Story Hooks: Exploring the minds of troubled students; encountering mind-altering experiences.
    8. Outdoor Adventure Club
      • Skills: Navigate, Survival
      • Story Hooks: Organizing trips into the nearby forests; discovering hidden places and strange happenings.
    9. Thespian Troupe of Caroline
      • Skills: Acting, Disguise
      • Story Hooks: Performing plays that have unexpected effects; blurring lines between roles and reality.
    10. Astronomy Enthusiasts of Caroline
      • Skills: Astronomy, Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Observing unexplained celestial events; contact with cosmic entities.
    11. Strategy Games Club
      • Skills: Strategy Game (Chess), Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Strategic contests with unusual stakes; deciphering patterns that hint at larger schemes.
    12. Caroline Art Collective
      • Skills: Art/Craft (Painting, Sculpture)
      • Story Hooks: Art that seems to come alive; students disappearing after creating certain pieces.
    13. Music Appreciation Group
      • Skills: Art/Craft (Music), Listen
      • Story Hooks: Composing or playing music that has strange effects; instruments with mysterious origins.
    14. Watashuka Historical Preservation Society
      • Skills: History, Library Use
      • Story Hooks: Efforts to preserve local history uncover hidden truths; dealing with those who want the past forgotten.
    15. Lakeview Cryptid Hunters
      • Skills: Natural World, Track
      • Story Hooks: Searching for legendary creatures said to inhabit local areas; encounters with the unknown.
    16. Philosophical Inquiry Club
      • Skills: Philosophy, Occult
      • Story Hooks: Discussions that lead to unsettling realizations; questioning reality itself.
    17. Language and Culture Exchange
      • Skills: Language (Other), Anthropology
      • Story Hooks: Connecting with international students; discovering ancient languages with hidden power.
    18. Self-Defense and Martial Arts Club
      • Skills: Fighting (Brawl), Dodge
      • Story Hooks: Learning techniques with unexpected origins; defending against unseen threats.
    19. Photography and Journalism Society
      • Skills: Photography, Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Capturing images that reveal more than meets the eye; investigating stories that others avoid.
    20. Our Earth Group
      • Skills: Biology, Survival
      • Story Hooks: Protecting local environments leads to strange discoveries; nature behaving unpredictably. Keeper's Note: Involvement in clubs can provide:
    • Skill Bonuses: Gain a +5% bonus to associated skills while active in the club.
    • Story Opportunities: Clubs can introduce unique quests, NPCs, and resources.
    • Potential Risks: Groups can grant mechanical benefits as well as possible contacts. But some clubs may be fronts for groups your character may not wish to draw attention from.

    By incorporating these elements, we aim to enrich the gameplay experience, providing ample opportunities for character development, fun, and immersive storytelling.
