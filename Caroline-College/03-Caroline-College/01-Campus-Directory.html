    Caroline College: Campus Directory

    • Prairie Hall
      • Built: 1856
      • Description: The oldest structure on campus, Prairie Hall was the first building constructed for the original Prairieville School. Now a historic museum, it houses artifacts from early Wisconsin life and stands as a testament to the school’s origins in education. The building sits atop an elaborate basement which houses most of the artifacts.
    • Founder's Hall
      • Built: 1862, relocated 1945
      • Description: Founder's Hall, originally constructed after the rebuilding of the Prairieville School in 1862, now serves as the college's alumni center. It was relocated in 1945 to make way for campus land expansion and construction.
    • Morgan Lafayette Liberal Arts Center
      • Built: 1950
      • Description: A bustling center for liberal arts, the Lafayette Center encourages interdisciplinary collaboration and intellectual discourse. Its modernist architecture was funded through significant post-war grants, supporting the growth of liberal education.
    • Duff Business Center of Excellence
      • Built: 1949
      • Student Nickname: DBC
      • Description: The Duff Business Center is home to the esteemed business program, offering real-world opportunities for students through partnerships with local industries. Its growth was fueled by post-WWII funding aimed at developing future leaders in business.
    • Rite Hall
      • Built: 1875
      • Student Nickname: Admin
      • Description: Serving as the administrative core of Caroline College, Rite Hall houses student services, faculty offices, and administrative departments. This stately building has long been the backdrop for important college events and student support services.
    • Brown Science Complex
      • Built: 1945-1954
      • Student Nickname: The Dungeon
      • Description: The Brown Science Complex consists of interconnected buildings dedicated to advancing scientific inquiry. The complex was developed with government support to enhance STEM education post-WWII.
      • Buildings Include:
        • Castelaz Building and Observatory (physical sciences): the crown jewel of the Brown complex, the observatory houses a stunning 30-inch refractor telescope, the Wright Telescope; one of the largest student-operated telescopes in the Midwest.
        • Gardner Mathematics Hall (mathematics and parapsychology): Gardner Hall is a  striking example of modern architectural prowess that houses a thriving mathematics department. At the heart of Gardner Mathematics Hall lies the Great Hall, a breathtaking three-story atrium with soaring ceilings and intricate glasswork that bathe the space in natural light. Here, students gather to collaborate on complex problems, attend guest lectures, and engage in spirited debates – all within an atmosphere of intellectual rigor and camaraderie.
        • Luttermoser Science Hall (biological and chemical sciences): The centerpiece of Luttermoser Science Hall is the Atrium of Discovery, a vast open-plan space that encourages interdisciplinary collaboration and exchange among faculty and students. Here, you'll find interactive exhibits, artwork inspired by scientific concepts, and even a living wall of plants.
        • The Obelisk of Nekhbet (monument): On permanent loan from Cairo University. Installed in 1954, the Obelisk stands as a symbol of the college’s commitment to global cultural awareness and intellectual exchange.
    • Scarlet Center for Women’s Studies
      • Built: 1970
      • Description: A focal point for research and study of gender studies, law, forensic science and social justice issues, the Scarlet Center is a progressive space dedicated to intellectual exploration. Its development was part of a post-war expansion to support diverse academic programs.
    • Adda F. Howie Center for Agricultural Theory
      • Built: 1958
      • Description: Named after a trailblazing figure in agricultural science, the Howie Center connects theoretical agricultural studies with practical applications. The center’s programs have been instrumental in modernizing regional agricultural practices.
    • C.D. Levi Agriculture Science Complex
      • Built: 1901
      • Student Nickname: The Farm
      • Description: Known for its extensive greenhouses and experimental farms, "The Farm" provides hands-on experience in agriculture. The complex’s expansion after WWII reflects the nation’s emphasis on sustainability and food security.
    • Lantern Memorial Park, Reflecting Pool, and Cemetery
      • Established: 1939
      • Student Nickname: The Swinging Tree
      • Description: A serene spot for reflection, Lantern Memorial Park commemorates the life of visiting artist Elijah Kane. It offers a peaceful place for students to gather, surrounded by nature and tranquility.
    • Prairie Dog Stadium
      • Built: 1914
      • Description: Home to the Prairie Dogs baseball team, this stadium fosters community spirit and competition. The facility is also used for various campus events, including intramural sports and large gatherings.
    • The Burrow
      • Built: 1968
      • Student Nickname: Student Center
      • Description: As the social hub of campus, The Burrow provides spaces for student organizations, recreational activities, and events. This lively center encourages students to engage with their peers and build lasting friendships.
    • Ho-Chunk Cultural Heritage Museum
      • Built: 1955
      • Student Nickname: HCC
      • Description: The HCC museum showcases the rich history and cultural contributions of Wisconsin's Ho-Chunk Nation. It serves as an educational space where students and visitors can learn about indigenous heritage.
    • S. Fields Building
      • Built: 1885
      • Student Nickname: SFB
      • Description: This building houses the departments of anthropology and sociology. It offers students the opportunity to explore societal structures and human behavior through comprehensive research programs.
    • Philip Love Aquatic Center
      • Built: 1975
      • Student Nickname: The Pool
      • Description: The newest facility on campus, the Philip Love Aquatic Center boasts both indoor and outdoor pools, offering opportunities for recreational swimming, water sports, and scuba certification.
    • Rutherford-Walsh Mann Track and Sport Field
      • Built: 1901
      • Description: A key athletic facility, this complex supports track and field events, intramural sports, and physical education classes. Its construction reflected the post-war emphasis on physical fitness and community well-being.
    • McElhenney Journalism School and Communications Center
      • Built: 1955
      • Description: A center for media and communications studies, McElhenney School trains students in modern journalism practices. The school’s graduates have gone on to work in national media outlets, reflecting its reputation for excellence.
    • Bowstein Center of Philosophy and Religious Research
      • Built: 1869
      • Description: The Bowstein Center engages students with global philosophical and religious traditions. The center’s vast library and connections with international scholars make it a vibrant hub for intellectual exchange.
    • Gale Veterinary Hospital and Training School
      • Built: 1957
      • Description: This hospital specializes in the treatment of large animals, providing students with hands-on veterinary training. It serves both the campus and the surrounding agricultural community.
    • Research Greenhouses (Plant Science Research)
      • Built: 1953
      • Description: The Research Greenhouses offer state-of-the-art facilities for plant science and agriculture. Students here work on projects related to sustainability, conservation, and crop development.
    • West Maintenance Building
      • Built: 1946
      • Student Nickname: The Dump
      • Description: Housing the campus facilities management team, this building ensures the maintenance and smooth operation of the entire campus, from heating systems to grounds keeping. A few industrial arts courses are even taught here. The students who choose to engage are given the opportunity to learn and help their school!
    • Campus Security Station
      • Built: 1888
      • Student Nickname: The Jail
      • Description: The security station monitors the campus 24/7, offering students and staff a sense of safety and peace of mind. Its modern surveillance systems keep the campus secure.
    • Fonseca Power Systems Research Facilities
      • Built: 1960
      • Student Nickname: The Nightlight
      • Description: This engineering research facility provides students with unparalleled opportunities in civil, electrical, and mechanical engineering. Its advanced labs and technology help foster the next generation of engineers.
    • Don Peachy Center for the Performing Arts
      • Built: 1956
      • Description: A vibrant space for theater and music, the Don Peachy Center is home to numerous student performances and cultural events. Its folk and ethnic music programs are renowned in the region.
    • Fine Arts Building
      • Built: 1899
      • Student Nickname: The Fab
      • Description: The Fine Arts Building, constructed in 1899, stands as one of the oldest structures on campus, housing studios, galleries, and classrooms for the college’s art programs. Originally named after a prominent local patron, its title was quietly changed in 1923 following a scandal that has since been buried, leaving it now known simply as the Fine Arts Building.
    • J. Sawyer Amphitheater
      • Built: 1937
      • Student Nickname: The Pit
      • Description: This expansive outdoor venue hosts concerts, guest speakers, and campus-wide events. Its open-air design provides a picturesque setting for graduation ceremonies.
    • Prairie Academy Teacher Training Center
      • Built: 1965
      • Student Nickname: The Academy
      • Description: The Academy is a center for teacher training, returning the college to its roots. The facility is also home to the E. Rowe School for the Gifted, providing specialized education for exceptional students.
    • Sterling Men’s Dormitory and E. Root Women’s Dormitory
      • Built: 1950
      • Description: These dormitories provide comfortable housing for students from all corners of the country. Their location in the heart of campus ensures students are close to academic and social life.
    • Dennis W. Dark Ecology Research Center
      • Built: 1961
      • Description: This research station offers students the opportunity to study the region’s native ecology, with advanced technology for weather tracking and ecological research. Advanced ecology and environmental laboratory work is performed here.
    • Annex Buildings
      • Built: 1863
      • Student Nickname: The Attics
      • Description: Originally constructed to house soldiers during the Civil War, these buildings are now used for storage. They stand as a reminder of the college's long history and connection to national events.
    • Qubit Tower
      • Built: 1968
      • Student Nickname: The Brain
      • Description: Qubit Tower is home to two of the most powerful supercomputers in the Midwest, offering students unique opportunities in computational research and data science.
    • Revival Study Center (Classical Studies)
      • Built: 1870
      • Description: A center for the study of classical languages, archology and ancient civilizations, this facility hosts a comprehensive collection of rare texts and artifacts, fostering academic excellence in classical studies.
    • J. W. Midnight Library
      • Built: 1955
      • Description: The campus library offers extensive resources for students, including rare books, archives, and study spaces. Librarians are available to assist students in their academic pursuits. Library science courses are offered in basement levels 1 and 2.
    • Sister Sarah’s Teaching Hospital
      • Built: 1927
      • Description: A teaching hospital providing top-notch medical services to the campus and community, as well as training opportunities for healthcare students in clinical settings. It offers coursework in pre-med as well as provide facilities for biological labs.
    • Applied Health and Nursing Center
      • Built: 1966
      • Description: The Applied Health and Nursing Center is a modern, multi-story facility that serves as the hub for Caroline College’s nursing and health education programs. Equipped with state-of-the-art simulation labs, clinical classrooms, and research spaces, the center provides hands-on training for students pursuing careers in healthcare. Its proximity to Watashuka’s teaching hospital ensures a seamless integration of academic learning with real-world clinical experience, preparing students for immediate entry into the healthcare field.
    • St. John's Lutheran Chapel
      • Built: 1890
      • Description: The oldest Lutheran chapel on a public college campus in the region, St. John’s offers classes in religious studies and serves the spiritual needs of the student body. Graduate work in comparative religion and philosophy is offered here.
    • [WCTU](WCTU Radio Station)
      • Overview: The campus radio station at Caroline College. It is housed in the McElhenney Journalism School. The station is run by a mix of students and fulltime staff. Fulltime staff includes:
        • Michelina Plum, who hosts the late-night show "Night Vibes." from 10:00pm to 5:00 am. The show focuses on classical musical to sleep and study to.
        • Albert Hawkins, who hosts the early morning news and Americana program "News and Roots" from 5:00 am to noon.
      • WCTU AM Radio (Public Radio On The Campus Of Caroline College)

    Clubs and Societies

    Below are 20 clubs and societies with you might engage with in and around the college. Each includes associated Call of Cthulhu skills.

    1. Great Lakes Archaeological Society
      • Skills: Archaeology, History
      • Story Hooks: Discovering Native American artifacts linked to ancient rituals; uncovering local historical sites.
    2. Caroline College Debate Club
      • Skills: Persuade, Fast Talk
      • Story Hooks: Debating controversial topics that stir up hidden tensions; uncovering faculty secrets.
    3. Peach Street Library Occult Studies Group
      • Skills: Occult, Library Use
      • Story Hooks: Access to obscure local folklore; accidental involvement with real supernatural forces.
    4. Folklore and Mythology Circle
      • Skills: Anthropology, History
      • Story Hooks: Exploring regional legends; finding connections between myths and current strange occurrences.
    5. Caroline Science Association
      • Skills: Science (choose one), Mechanical Repair
      • Story Hooks: Experiments affected by mysterious phenomena; encountering anomalies in nature.
    6. Literary Appreciation Society
      • Skills: Language (Own or Other), Art/Craft (Literature)
      • Story Hooks: Uncovering hidden messages in classic literature; meeting reclusive authors with dark secrets.
    7. Psychology and Wellness Club
      • Skills: Psychology, Psychoanalysis
      • Story Hooks: Exploring the minds of troubled students; encountering mind-altering experiences.
    8. Outdoor Adventure Club
      • Skills: Navigate, Survival
      • Story Hooks: Organizing trips into the nearby forests; discovering hidden places and strange happenings.
    9. Thespian Troupe of Caroline
      • Skills: Acting, Disguise
      • Story Hooks: Performing plays that have unexpected effects; blurring lines between roles and reality.
    10. Astronomy Enthusiasts of Caroline
      • Skills: Astronomy, Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Observing unexplained celestial events; contact with cosmic entities.
    11. Strategy Games Club
      • Skills: Strategy Game (Chess), Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Strategic contests with unusual stakes; deciphering patterns that hint at larger schemes.
    12. Caroline Art Collective
      • Skills: Art/Craft (Painting, Sculpture)
      • Story Hooks: Art that seems to come alive; students disappearing after creating certain pieces.
    13. Music Appreciation Group
      • Skills: Art/Craft (Music), Listen
      • Story Hooks: Composing or playing music that has strange effects; instruments with mysterious origins.
    14. Watashuka Historical Preservation Society
      • Skills: History, Library Use
      • Story Hooks: Efforts to preserve local history uncover hidden truths; dealing with those who want the past forgotten.
    15. Lakeview Cryptid Hunters
      • Skills: Natural World, Track
      • Story Hooks: Searching for legendary creatures said to inhabit local areas; encounters with the unknown.
    16. Philosophical Inquiry Club
      • Skills: Philosophy, Occult
      • Story Hooks: Discussions that lead to unsettling realizations; questioning reality itself.
    17. Language and Culture Exchange
      • Skills: Language (Other), Anthropology
      • Story Hooks: Connecting with international students; discovering ancient languages with hidden power.
    18. Self-Defense and Martial Arts Club
      • Skills: Fighting (Brawl), Dodge
      • Story Hooks: Learning techniques with unexpected origins; defending against unseen threats.
    19. Photography and Journalism Society
      • Skills: Photography, Spot Hidden
      • Story Hooks: Capturing images that reveal more than meets the eye; investigating stories that others avoid.
    20. Our Earth Group
      • Skills: Biology, Survival
      • Story Hooks: Protecting local environments leads to strange discoveries; nature behaving unpredictably.

    Keeper's Note: Involvement in clubs can provide:

    • Skill Bonuses: Gain a +5% bonus to associated skills while active in the club.
    • Story Opportunities: Clubs can introduce unique quests, NPCs, and resources.
    • Potential Risks: Groups can grant mechanical benefits as well as possible contacts. But some clubs may be fronts for groups your character may not wish to draw attention from.
