    Potion-Based Magic (5e)

    Potion-Based Magic (5e)

    Potion-Based Magic Rules for the World

    OK neophyte, even if you don't want to take up the Craft, you should know a thing or two about potions. Sure you may have had someone who could mix up a draught or grind out a poultice back home. But out in the wider world you'll find that people are doing things your old granddad never thought could be done with herbs and viscera. So sit down, and listen closely, for I've seen the wonders and horrors of magic in this world, and I'd hate to see you stumble into something you can't handle. Potion-making, you see, is an ancient art — a careful mix of science, intuition, and a bit of plain ol' luck. It’s not some finicky old civilization piece of junk that’ll blow up in your face because you looked at it wrong. No, potion brewing is reliable, and anyone — human or otherwise — can learn the craft if they’ve got the patience and a little bit of gear. The Craft is about rolling up your sleeves, grinding herbs, and hoping you don’t blow yourself up in the process.

    Crafting Potions by Level

    Potion-making is a skill that takes time to master. The more experienced you get, and the better equipment you gather, the stronger the concoctions you can brew. But remember, higher-level potions require more elaborate gear — it’s not just about your skill but also the tools you have at your disposal. Here’s a breakdown of what you can manage as you grow:

    • Minor Potions: Simple stuff, really. These are the kind of potions that can heal a small cut or help you see the unseen — nothing too fancy, but they'll save your hide more times than you'd think. The equipment needed is modest and can usually be found in any small village, and can even be carried along with your other gear. Hell, it might be your other gear; cooking pot, hammer for breaking up minerals can put a hurt on a foe, etc.
      • Equivalent to 1st-level spells. In general, requires 1-10 sp worth of components (many of which can be foraged) and basic brewing equipment.
    • Basic Potions: Now we're getting somewhere. These brews pack a bit more punch, like giving you a bark-like skin or boosting your strength for a while. The equipment can still fit in a backpack, though compromises might have to be made; it might be more challenging to make what you want, or it might take longer, with equipment you can travel with.
      • Equivalent to 2nd-level spells. In general, requires 1-10 gp worth of components and more specialized equipment.
    • Greater Potions: These are the potions you take when things get serious. Fire in a bottle, quite literally, or a brew that lets you shrug off blows like they were not more than insect annoying ya'. Crafting these requires a dedicated workspace — you can't just make these on the road... mostly.
      • Equivalent to 3rd-level spells. In general, requires 10-100 gp of components and a dedicated space.
    • Superior Potions: Not easy to make, and you’ll need rare ingredients and a specialized workspace. You might need a full alchemical lab to get these right, and the higher the level, the more elaborate the equipment required.
      • Equivalent to 4th-level spells. In general, requires 100-500 gp of components along with specialized workspaces and tools.
    • Major Potions: The stuff of legends. Takes a week or more of careful tending, a stash of exotic ingredients, and equipment worth more than village might earn in a year. These potions often need unique components, and the process is so delicate that even the best gear can barely suffice.
      • Equivalent to 5th-level spells. In general, requires a minimum of 250 gp worth of unique components, usually not available for purchase (i.e. the stuff of quests), and highly specialized workspaces and tools.

    Crafting Requirements

    Time Required:

    Crafting a potion takes time — and patience. And when I say a full day, ya' better believe it will be every part of that 24 hours. You might not be cutting and grinding every moment, but you will be watching pots and temperatures and waiting! The stronger the potion, the more time you’ll need. In general, here's a rule of thumb. But believe me, some recipes take longer.

    • Minor Potions: 1 hour of work.
    • Basic Potions: 4 hours.
    • Greater Potions: 1 full day.
    • Superior Potions: 3 days.
    • Major Potions: 7 days. A week of careful brewing, with no distractions.

    Ingredients:

    Of course, you’ll need ingredients — some common, some rare. The rarer the potion, the more costly the ingredients:

    • Minor Potions: 1-10 silver pennies (sp) worth of herbs and materials. Anyone with basic foraging skills could find what they need with a bit of effort.
    • Basic Potions: 1-10 gp worth of ingredients. These might require a bit more specialized components, but they can usually be found in a small town or brewed from more common items with time.
    • Greater Potions: 10-100 gp worth of ingredients. These require rare herbs, minerials or items that are harder to come by.
    • Superior Potions: 100-500 gold pieces (gp) worth of rare herbs and alchemical oddities. You may need to travel to a distant city or find an apothecary with connections to obtain these.
    • Major Potions: 250 gp and up worth of the most exotic ingredients you can find. These ingredients aren't sold in shops — they're the kind of treasures you get only through adventure and risk. And trust me, it's a lot less risky than messing with some old civilization artifact.

    Equipment Needed:

    The equipment required for potion-making grows more elaborate as the potions become more powerful. Here's a breakdown:

    • Minor Potions: Basic gear — a simple mortar and pestle, a small cauldron, and a few glass vials. The kind of kit you could easily carry in a backpack or find in a small village.
    • Basic Potions: More refined tools — an alembic for distillation, measuring scales for precision, and glass beakers. While still portable, carrying this gear means compromises might have to be made. Its better to do this work in a stable place; but we all know that isn't always possible.
    • Greater Potions: Requires a dedicated workspace. You’ll need a fireproof workbench, a larger cauldron, distillation coils, and retorts. Setting this up on the road is next to impossible.
      • Now, I say next to impossible but I know the traveling peoples who live near a Zone's border will set up their workspaces on specialized wagons. Smiths, Crafters, Weavers, you name it. When your lands shift on ya', you best be ready to take your trade with you. But even here they don't tend to 'brew and ride' unless they want to poison their party or blow up a horse. 
    • Superior Potions: Needs a full alchemical setup, including specialized tools like an athanor (a type of furnace), vessels for containment, and multiple burners to keep mixtures at specific temperatures. This is lab work, not traveling work. Solid ground is not optional here.
    • Major Potions: The equipment needed for this work, well it's worth more than many villages could earn in a year. You’ll need exotic glassware, gold-trimmed alembics, crystal decanters, and even some items that can only be crafted by master blacksmiths, obtained through rare finds, or, and I hate to say it, but you might even need some old civilization tools. This work is beyond me; in skill and cost. If you want to brew at this level, a fully equipped lab is essential, and sometimes even that isn’t enough — you might need specialized equipment that are only used by one recipe!

    Crafting Skill Checks

    Initial Crafting Check:

    Potion-making isn’t just about having the right recipe — it’s about skill, experience, and a touch of luck. (When crafting a potion, you’ll need to make a check using Arcana, Nature, or Medicine based on the recipe. The DC for the initial crafting is based on the potion you’re making. The following are guidelines, see the potion being attempted for details.)

    • Minor Potions: Easy enough, unless you’re all thumbs. (DC 10)
    • Basic Potions: A little trickier, but nothing a bit of practice can’t handle. (DC 13)
    • Greater Potions: You’ll want to focus for these. (DC 15)
    • Superior Potions: One wrong move, and you’re in for a nasty surprise. (DC 17)
    • Major Potions: Even the best of us mess these up sometimes. (DC 20)

    Use Check:

    Even if you brew the potion perfectly, there’s always a chance it doesn’t quite turn out the way you expect. (When using a potion, make a Constitution Saving Throw for potions consumed, a Wisdom Saving Throw for potions used but not consumed, to see if the potion is still viable. The DC for this check is based on the potion level. The following are guidelines, see the potion being attempted for details.)

    • Minor Potions: Most of the time, these work just fine. (DC 5)
    • Basic Potions: A small chance of spoilage. (DC 8)
    • Greater Potions: Sometimes, the magic just doesn’t hold. (DC 10)
    • Superior Potions: A bit riskier, but still usually fine. (DC 12)
    • Major Potions: There’s always a risk with the truly powerful brews. (DC 15)

    Failure Consequences:

    Always be careful using a product of the Craft; bad things have been known to happen. (On a failed crafting check, the ingredients are wasted, and nothing comes of it. If you fail by 5 or more, there could be a mishap — maybe a small explosion, or some accidental poisoning. On a failed use check, the potion either has no effect or a mild negative effect e.g., nausea, headache, etc. for an hour. )

    Potion Potency and Expiration

    Potency Duration:

    Potions don’t last forever, but some can hold their magic longer than others:

    • Minor Potions: Good for 1 week if stored properly.
    • Basic Potions: Last 2 weeks when sealed.
    • Greater Potions: Remain potent for 1 month if undisturbed.
    • Superior Potions: Can last for a year — though you’d best keep them in a cool, dark place.
    • Major Potions: Well, no one knows for sure. Might be forever, might not. I haven’t heard of one going bad yet, but who’s to say?

    Storage:

    Keep them sealed and safe. If they get exposed to air or something contaminates them, their shelf-life drops to 24 hours. I’ve seen adventurers take a swig from a contaminated potion — not a pretty sight.

    Using Potions

    • Action to Use: In general, using a potion takes an action. But sometimes it might take more set-up time (e.g. mixing active ingredients, setting up a smoker, etc.). Consult the recipe for details. And if you have to throw one, — like a fire potion — you have to make sure you hit your target; or at least close enough to it (ranged attack roll and splash effects may apply).
    • Carrying Limit: You can only carry so many. Carry too many and you’re asking for trouble — breakages, leaks, and other 'mishaps'. (The number of potions you can haul around is equal to your 5x Constitution modifier (min 1). Some equipment may allow you to carry more.)

    Limitations on Available Spells

    Not all magic can be bottled up, no matter how hard you try. Let me put it this way: potions are useful, but they have their limits. Ain't no potion going to do what those dangerous old civilization artifacts can do. I've seen those things put up invisible walls, ones no sword could cut through. That's not the kind of magic you get from brewing a potion.

    • Anything that needs concentration — like shaping shadows — forget it. Potions just don't have the finesse for that. If it’s needs constant effort to maintain, it’s beyond what a potion can do.
    • Magic that pulls from your soul or speaks directly to the gods or things like that, well that’s personal; you won’t find that in a bottle. Potions might give you a boost, but they won't let you glimpse the divine or turn back time.

    Example Potions

    • Elixir of Health (Minor Potion): Nothing fancy, but it'll keep you standing. It will even heal humans when the leave the Sheltered Route. (Restores 1d8 + character's level HP).
    • Lotion of Bark (Basic Potion): Good for when you’re feeling a little too squishy. It'll toughen up your skin a bit so a knife or sword will but scratch you. (Grants AC 16 for 1 hour).
    • Preparation of Fire (Greater Potion): A ball of fire in the palm of your hands; dangerous stuff but very satisfying. Just make sure you’re not in the blast zone. (Can be thrown up to 30 feet, dealing 8d6 fire damage in a 20-foot radius; DC 15 Dex save for half).
    • Salve to Harden the Skin (Superior Potion): Every potion can be goosed up a bit. This stuff is like the Lotion of Bark, but better (and less smelly). It makes you feel like a walking fortress! (Grants resistance to non-magical bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage for 1 hour.)
    • Vapors of Safe Passage (Major Potion): Now, this one’s a marvel. I don't know if its true but I heard from a traveling holy figure, in a bar in Ashenfort, that she could make it. She says that if you, and up to 5 friends, breathe in the smoke from this potion, I guess you smoke it, you all fall into a deep sleep. In your dream, you’ll walk to a place you know — when you wake up, you’re there. Takes about a minute to work, and you will be out cold for 8 hours; nothing is going to wake you up. But the crazy part of this? Your body gets up and walks while you get all rested. If you’re not strong enough (DC 15 Con save), you might stay asleep for a while longer than other folks (1d4 hours added to the time you are asleep, gain no benefit of the long rest, and take a level of exhaustion).

    These are the ways of potion-based magic in the world that I know of; different folks do things differently. The Craft is not for the faint of heart, but if you’re willing to put in the time, it’ll serve you well. Just remember — magic always comes with a price, and it’s usually one you don’t see until it’s too late.
